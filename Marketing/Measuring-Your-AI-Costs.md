# Measuring your AI costs — why it matters, and what to expect

> **TL;DR:** If you're deploying LLMs at any scale, the model / router / policy choice is now a material line item — but most organizations still make it on vendor benchmarks and gut feel. Instar is an open-source harness (Apache 2.0) that runs your real traffic through candidate configurations and produces defensible cost / quality / latency reports. This document explains why measurement matters, what a serious evaluation actually looks like, and where Instar fits.

## Part 1 — Why you need this

### The stakes rose while nobody was looking

A year ago, LLM spend was a rounding error and the choice of model didn't matter much. Now:

- Your inference bill is a real line item, growing faster than anyone is renegotiating it.
- The gap between "the cheapest model that would work for this" and "what you're actually using" is often several-fold on cost — sometimes with a *quality improvement* on the cheaper choice.
- New models and routers ship monthly. Every one of them claims to be better on your workload.
- The people who first picked a model at your company have often moved on; the choice is now legacy, and no one wants to touch it because no one wants to break it.

Meanwhile the decision-making inputs haven't scaled. Most organizations still decide routing and model choices based on some combination of: vendor benchmarks (measured on someone else's workload), Twitter / conference talks (measured on demos), and internal gut feel (measured on the last three requests someone happened to look at).

That's an expensive way to run a growing part of your operating budget.

### What "measurement" actually needs to mean

A measurement that's worth acting on — one that survives a CFO review, an ML team turnover, and the next model release — needs to be:

- **On your own traffic**, not on a benchmark someone else designed.
- **Comparable across configurations** — same request, same rubric, different model or router, apples to apples.
- **Reproducible** — hermetic, seeded, pinned model IDs — so the report you show today can be verified in six months.
- **Multi-dimensional** — cost *and* quality *and* latency, because trading one against another is the whole game.
- **Delivered as evidence, not opinion** — the artifact should be a report a stakeholder can read, not a story someone tells.

Few existing tools produce this shape of output. Eval platforms (Promptfoo, Braintrust, DeepEval) are excellent at prompt-level and unit-level comparison. Observability tools (Langfuse, Helicone, Arize) tell you what happened in production but not what *would* happen under a different configuration. Routers (OpenRouter, LiteLLM, Portkey) decide production requests but don't tell you whether the decisions were correct.

Instar is designed to fill the gap between these — a *measurement harness*, workload in, evidence out.

### Why open source

Because the answer needs to be independently verifiable. If your model-choice report is generated by a closed proprietary tool from the same vendor selling you the models, the report is not evidence — it's marketing. Instar is Apache 2.0. No telemetry. No lock-in. Your data stays yours; your report is reproducible by anyone with the same fixtures.

## Part 2 — What to expect when you start evaluating

The first serious evaluation takes weeks, not days — mostly because *defining the question well* is the hard part. Here is what the process actually looks like.

### Step 1 — Scope the workload (1–2 weeks)

Before you can measure, you need to know what you're measuring. Practically:

- Which end-user flow, feature, or workflow are you evaluating? Pick one, not five.
- What does a real request in that flow look like? Sample 100–500 real requests, anonymized.
- What does "quality" mean here? Sometimes it's answer correctness. Sometimes it's brand voice. Sometimes it's format compliance. Usually it's 2–3 dimensions.

The output of this step is a *workload fixture* — a replayable set of representative requests — and a *rubric* — a written definition of "good" specific enough that an LLM judge can score consistently.

Expect this step to surprise you. Most organizations discover that their "AI use case" is actually three different workloads with three different quality definitions, and that the current model choice was only ever reasonable for one of them.

### Step 2 — Define the candidate space (a few days)

Which routers, models, and policies do you want to compare? A reasonable first pass:

- 2–4 candidate models (current model + one cheaper + one premium).
- 1–2 routing strategies (e.g., always-current vs. a cost-quality classifier).
- The same rubric across all of them.

Don't try to compare everything on the first pass. A narrow candidate space keeps the report legible.

### Step 3 — Run the harness (a day, once set up)

With workload + rubric + candidate space in hand, you run the harness. Instar produces a report showing:

- Cost per request, per candidate.
- Quality distribution, per candidate, per rubric dimension.
- Latency (p50, p95, p99), per candidate.
- Where the trade-offs are — which candidates dominate, which are strictly dominated, and where the Pareto frontier sits.

### Step 4 — Read the report honestly (a week)

The report is not the decision. It is the *input* to a decision. Expect to find:

- One or two candidates that dominate the incumbent on cost with equal or better quality. (This is the common case.)
- Quality regressions on subsets you didn't expect the incumbent to be good at. (Worth investigating.)
- Latency behaviors that break assumptions, especially on newer models.
- Genuinely ambiguous cases where the trade-off is a business call, not a technical one. (This is where a leader is needed.)

### Step 5 — Decide, then re-measure (ongoing)

The point of measurement is not a one-shot decision. It is a repeatable process. New models ship monthly. Your workload drifts. What was optimal in Q1 may not be in Q3. A measurement pipeline that runs quarterly, or per major model release, keeps the decision current — and gives you the receipts when someone asks why you chose what you chose.

## What Instar is, and isn't

**Is:** an open-source harness for measuring cost, quality, and latency of LLM workloads on your own traffic.

**Isn't:**

- **Not a router.** Instar tells you which routing choices *would* have paid off; it doesn't decide production requests. Use OpenRouter, LiteLLM, Portkey, Kong, or Cloudflare AI Gateway for that.
- **Not a general eval platform.** For prompt-level and unit-level evaluation across many benchmarks, Promptfoo, Braintrust, and DeepEval are broader.
- **Not a trace / observability store.** Langfuse, Helicone, Arize, and LangSmith own that.
- **Not a hosted service.** No SaaS. You run it (or your partner runs it for you).
